Didcot power cut what happened to the power station and when did the outage start? The Sun

THOUSANDS of homes in Didcot were left without power after the town's infamous power station was demolished.

Let's take a closer look at what happened.

When was Didcot power station demolished?

The three cooling towers were brought to the ground at the Oxfordshire town on the morning of August 18.

Plumes of smoke and dust were sent billowing into the air after the "controlled" explosion at 7am.

The 375ft high towers, previously voted Britain's third worst eyesore, have previously divided public opinion.

Bill Service, the Mayor of Didcot, previously said he had mixed feelings, saying today would "mark the end of one era for the town".

When was the power outage?

Moments after the cooling towers were destroyed, 40,000 homes and traffic lights lost power .

However, Scottish and Southern Electricity Networks (SSE) insisted the fault "wasn't linked to the demolition".

Instead, the Oxford Mail has reported a drone may have hit power lines, causing a fire.

SSE confirmed a fault had affected thousands in the area, saying: "Shortly after 7am this morning, SSEN received reports of damage to its network at Sutton Courtenay, following the demolition of the nearby Didcot Power Station.

"SSEN engineers attended site to make the situation safe and power was fully restored to the 40,000 customers affected by 8.20am.

"An investigation is underway as to the cause of the incident and SSEN is working with all relevant authorities. Further details will be shared once they are known."

Footage from the scene has shown power lines on fire.

Locals had gathered to watch as the last three cooling towers at the Didcot A plant were brought down this morning.

Why was the power station destroyed?

Didcot A had been commissioned in 1964, connected to the national grid in 1970.

It was ultimately decommissioned in 2013 when new EU reduced emission rules were introduced.

The station was hit with tragedy in 2016 when four men were killed as they worked in the boiler house.

Specialist firm Brown and Mason were called in to bring down the site.

The 655ft (199.5m) chimney will be demolished in autumn.
